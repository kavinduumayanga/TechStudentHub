How to Create a Professional Internship CV (Step-by-Step)

A strong internship CV isn’t about fancy design. It’s about clarity: a recruiter should understand who you are, what you can do, and what you’ve achieved in under 30 seconds. This guide gives you a simple structure, bullet formulas, and a checklist you can reuse for every application.

What recruiters scan first

For internship roles, reviewers usually skim in this order:

  1. Headline + education: your degree, year, and focus.
  2. Skills: relevant tools/technologies (not everything you’ve ever tried).
  3. Experience / projects: evidence you can execute work.
  4. Clarity: consistent formatting, short bullets, no walls of text.

Your job is to make this scan easy.

A simple internship CV structure

Use a one-page CV unless the internship explicitly asks for more.

Section 1: Header

  • Your name
  • City/country (optional)
  • Email + phone (if you use phone)
  • LinkedIn / portfolio (if available)

Section 2: Education

Include your degree, university/school, and expected graduation. Add 1–2 relevant modules only if they match the internship.

Section 3: Skills

Split skills into groups so they’re scannable:

  • Tools: e.g., Excel, Git, Figma
  • Programming: e.g., Python, JavaScript (only what you can use)
  • Soft skills: keep it short and evidenced in bullets

How to write strong bullet points

A good bullet is specific and measurable (even if the number is small).

Use this formula

Action verb + what you did + tool/skill + result.

  • Weak: “Worked on a project.”
  • Better: “Built a simple expense tracker in Excel with validation rules and charts to summarize monthly spending.”
  • Best: “Built an Excel expense tracker using validation rules and pivot charts, reducing manual categorization time by ~30 minutes/week.”

Projects section: how to prove skills

Projects are often the strongest evidence for interns. For each project, include:

  • What it is (one sentence)
  • Your role (what you personally did)
  • Tech/tools used
  • Outcome (what improved, what you shipped, what you learned)

If you have a portfolio link, ensure the pages load fast and don’t require login.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Too many skills with no proof (list fewer, show more)
  • Long paragraphs (use bullets and spacing)
  • Inconsistent dates/formatting (looks careless)
  • Copying job description keywords without relevant examples

Final checklist before you apply

  • One page (unless requested otherwise)
  • Consistent formatting (dates, headings, bullet style)
  • Links open correctly (portfolio, LinkedIn)
  • No spelling errors (read it out loud once)
  • Tailored top skills to the internship (small edits per role)
